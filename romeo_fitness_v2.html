After play · See Night Stretch routine
Every night before sleep. 15–20 minutes. Dim the lights. Slow breathing. This routine builds flexibility over time, speeds up muscle recovery, and significantly improves sleep quality. Do in bed or on your bedroom floor.
Full night routine · 15–20 min
1
Supine knee hug
45s each side · in bed
▾
Lie on your back. Pull one knee gently to your chest with both hands. Hold, breathe slowly, feel the lower back and glute release. Switch sides. Start here every night — it's the gentlest opener and instantly releases lower back tension from a day of sitting or playing.

As you breathe out, pull the knee very slightly closer. Don't force it.
Lower back · glutes · hip release
2
Figure-4 glute stretch
60s each side · in bed
▾
Lie on back, knees bent. Cross right ankle over left knee (like a figure 4). Either stay here or pull the left thigh toward your chest — you'll feel a deep stretch in the right glute and hip. This is the most effective stretch for piriformis tightness which causes a lot of lower back and hip pain in padel players.

The deeper the glute stretch, the better your lateral movement on court.
Piriformis · glutes · hip external rotation
3
Supine hip flexor (psoas) stretch
60s each side · floor or bed edge
▾
Sit on the edge of your bed. Lie back and let one leg hang off the side toward the floor while the other knee stays bent and pulled toward chest. Feel the front of the hanging hip stretch. This is the single most important flexibility unlock for court movement and lower back health. Most people who sit during the day have extremely tight hip flexors.

If you can only do one stretch from this list, do this one.
Hip flexors · psoas · lower back · court movement
4
Lying quad stretch
45s each side · in bed
▾
Lie on your side. Bend the top knee and hold your ankle behind you, pulling heel toward your glute. Keep hips stacked, don't twist. You'll feel the front of the thigh stretch deeply. After 3 hours of padel your quads are your most overworked muscle — this prevents the next-day stiffness you felt this week.

Breathe slowly and hold without bouncing. The stretch deepens naturally as you relax.
Quadriceps · knee tracking · post-padel recovery
5
Supine hamstring stretch (towel)
45s each side · in bed
▾
Lie on back. Loop a towel or bedsheet around one foot. Straighten the leg and gently pull it toward you using the towel — keep the knee as straight as comfortable. The other leg stays flat on the bed. This is the safest hamstring stretch you can do — no forward bending that strains the lower back.

Don't force the leg up. Let it rise naturally to where you feel tension, then hold there.
Hamstrings · sciatic nerve · lower back
6
Calf + Achilles stretch against wall
60s each side · standing at wall
▾
Stand facing your bedroom wall. One foot back, heel flat on floor, lean forward keeping back leg straight — stretches the calf. Then slightly bend the back knee — this targets the deeper Achilles and soleus. This is your #1 cramp prevention stretch. Do it every single night regardless of whether you played padel.

This is the stretch that will stop the overnight calf cramps you've been getting.
Calves · Achilles · cramp prevention · ankle mobility
7
Thoracic rotation (book opener)
10 reps each side · in bed
▾
Lie on your side with knees stacked and bent at 90°. Extend both arms forward. Take the top arm and slowly rotate it overhead and toward the bed behind you, following your hand with your eyes. Hold 3 seconds at the end range, return. This opens the mid-back (thoracic spine) which directly unlocks shoulder rotation for padel swings and relieves upper back tension from sitting.

Move only as far as comfortable — this gets dramatically better within 2–3 weeks.
Thoracic spine · shoulder rotation · upper back tension
8
Child's pose (floor or bed)
90s continuous
▾
Kneel and sit back toward your heels. Reach arms forward on the bed/floor and lower your forehead down. Let the entire back decompress. If your hips don't reach your heels, place a pillow between them. This position decompresses the entire spine after a day of being upright, releases the lower back, and calms the nervous system — which directly improves sleep quality.

Focus on slow, deep belly breathing here. This is your transition into sleep mode.
Full spine · lower back · nervous system calm · sleep quality
9
Legs-up relaxation pose
3–5 min · in bed against headboard
▾
Lie on your back and rest your legs up against the headboard or wall — legs straight up, relaxed. Close your eyes, breathe slowly. This is a passive recovery technique that drains lactic acid buildup from the legs, reduces inflammation, and reliably reduces overnight cramp risk.

This is the best thing you can do specifically for your calf cramp problem. It requires zero effort — you're literally just lying there. Stay 3–5 minutes before settling into sleep position.
Leg recovery · lactic acid drainage · cramp prevention · sleep
